About Me

I'm currently a graduate student completing my final year at The Ohio State University. I received my Bachelor's in Physics from the University of Tennessee, Knoxville, in 2020.

I was born and grew up in Franklin, Tennessee. I've always been fascinated with math and sciences, though astronomy truly captured my interest like no other. Like other kids, I wanted to be an astronaut, until my brother informed me that astrophysicists were the ones actually doing the math. From that day, my course was set.

I have a tendency to pick up various passions beyond astronomy as well. I love to cook and often find myself diving into culinary science, learning the physical processes that go into how we make our food taste good. I also love gaming in all forms: board games, TTRPGs, video games, etc. The best pastimes are ones you get to sink your teeth into and really spend some time thinking about. I find it so much fun to try and learn new complex mechanics and their intricacies.

Research Experience

I'm interested in red giants! There is so much to learn about their evolution and structure, and so much they can tell us about the galaxy! Most of my work involves carbon and nitrogen, notable mixing tracers, to learn about red giants throughout our Galaxy.

[C/N] throughout the RGB

At the base of the red giant branch, there is a large mixing event called the First Dredge-Up. This alters a star's surface carbon and nitrogen abundances in a mass-dependent way: more massive stars mix more deeply, altering their surface abundances more. We were able to exploit modern asteroseismic datasets, namely APOKASC3, to quantify this change and track how carbon and nitrogen change through this event. By examining the abundances of stars before the dredge-up and stars after, we could also show the impact that variations in the birth mixture have on the observed abundances. Beyond this, we were also able to follow the abundances further through RGB evolution, showcasing where additional mixing happens and providing a map to some of the most complicated abundances to understand in a star's spectra.

Galactic Archaeology and [C/N]

[C/N] can be used as a proxy for a star's mass, which means for red giants, it is also a proxy for age. Exploiting this, we quantified the relationship between [C/N] and age, as well as where it can be applied, and where it breaks down. Using this relationship, we were able to obtain ages for the hundreds of thousands of stars in APOGEE DR17 and look into the past 10 Gyr of our Galaxy's evolution.

Asteroseismology vs. [C/N]

Differences between [C/N]-based masses and asteroseismic masses are more than just differences from errors. There is actually a lot that those differences can tell us. This work is ongoing, so I don't want to spoil it yet, but it is super exciting. Stay Tuned!

Teaching Experience

Education is very important to me. I am constantly attempting to learn more about pedagogy and evidence-based teaching practices to help my students learn as well as possible. As part of this, I am pursuing the College and University Teaching Graduate Certificate at OSU, which I plan to complete at the same time I complete my Ph.D.

I am also fortunate enough to have been given multiple opportunities to teach while at OSU. I have assisted in teaching a large variety of undergraduate astronomy courses at OSU: I have helped with our Life in the Universe and Cosmology courses, and spent 3 years running the lab sections for our From Planets to Cosmos course. These experiences have helped me learn how much I love connecting with and teaching students, and directly inspired me to write my own classes.

At the moment, I have designed and taught two courses at OSU (though they are very closely related): Order of Magnitude and Advanced Order of Magnitude. Inspired by the graduate course of the same name, the class focuses on helping students in physics and astronomy learn how to think about problems broadly and tackle questions even when they think they don't have the tools to solve them. In addition, I make sure to work in practice with lots of skills that are very helpful in physics and astronomy but rarely formally taught, such as dimensional analysis or integral approximation. All wrapped up into goofy problems that have students laughing while they are learning.
